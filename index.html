CoGeneration

DEG offers affordable and dependable CoGeneration (CHP) systems along with conservation measure to increase your facilities bottom-line. CoGeneration also known as Combined Heat and Power (CHP), is the production of two kinds of energy –electricity and heat – from a single source of fuel. CHP replaces the traditional purchasing of electricity from the local electric utility and separately purchasing natural gas or other fossil fuels to burn in a boiler to produce heat. Combined Cooling Heating and Power (CCHP) takes this concept one-step further. The useful heat recovered from the CCHP application can be used to supply space and process heating and cooling needs. This is commonly referred to as CCHP or Tri-Generation. The recovered heat is utilized by an absorption cycle refrigeration machine, the resulting chilled water is sent to process or space cooling circuits. Heat not utilized in the absorber can be sent to the heating circuit where it is further used for domestic, process or space-heating as needed. DEG manages every phase of the CHP/CCHP process - from analysis to project design to financing and through to development/construction and installation.

Renewable Energy

Renewable energy is used to describe sources of energy or power that have the capacity to replenish themselves. Renewable energy is sometimes called infinite energy, because it relies on never ending energy sources. Renewable energy is also considered clean or green energy, because it does not produce toxins or pollutants that are harmful to the environment in the same manner that non-renewable energy does. The world is converting to renewable-clean energy sources to provide power for facilities, which lowers their costs and lets them function in a more environment friendly way. Buildings and facilities most commonly use Solar, Wind and Geothermal as renewable energy. Wind power is the most common form of renewable energy, by 2012 more than 5% of US electricity production will be from wind. Wind electricity is created when the wind energy turns blades connected to a turbine (a shaft & gears) which powers an electric generator. Wind power has a potentially infinite energy supply and a number of advantages to its use. Wind is free and in infinite supply making an affordable energy source. Wind electricity does not produce any pollutants or green house gases. Solar power is another renewable energy source, where the energy from the sun is directly harnessed in order to produce either heat (Thermal) or electricity (PV) energy. Sunlight is in limitless supply so long as it is shining, and is an excellent renewable energy source. Some areas of the world cannot rely on solar power because their weather and climate are not conducive to its use if long periods of cloudiness are present. Overall however, solar power is an endlessly free, clean energy source that does not pollute the environment or contribute to global warming. Solar Thermal captures the heat from sunlight and transfers it to a conducting material usually a fluid that in turn transfers the heat to storage or a directly into buildings mechanical systems where heat is needed. Solar PV-(Photovoltaics) turns photons from Sunlight directly into DC electricity and usually collected in panels that can be mounted individually or stung together by the thousands. Geothermal or Ground-source heat pumps (GSHP) is the name for a broad category of space conditioning systems that employ a geothermal resource – the ground, groundwater, or surface water – as both a heat source and sink. GSHPs use a reversible refrigeration cycle to provide either heating or cooling to storage or a directly into buildings mechanical systems where heat is needed.
